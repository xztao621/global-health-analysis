Introduction

Why investigate global health data?

Individual Health

Our lifestyles often have a significant impact on our well-being. What factors in our lifestyles lead to long and healthy lives?

Global Policy

We can use this data to understand public health differences across countries and regions. The results will also be more generalizable than in just a single country.

Unseen Solutions

A lot of health research takes place on a microscopic level where it may not be directly relevant to human health. What trends can we uncover at a macrosopic level?

Machine Learning

Predicting life expectancy using socioeconomic and health indicators

Data Preprocessing & Standardization

Prior to modeling, all continuous features were standardized to have means of 0 and variances of 1. This ensures comparability across predictors of different scales and improves optimization stability for linear models. An example of a standardized distribution of a feature is shown to the right.

Missing values were handled using previously established filtering criteria, retaining only columns with at most 50% missingness.

Scikit-learn was then used to split the data into 20% testing and 80% for training the models below.

Linear Regression

A baseline linear regression model was used to assess relationships between the predictors and life expectancy. Model performance was evaluated using RMSE and R² on the 20% of the data used for testing.

While showing promising accuracy, the linear model struggled to capture nonlinear effects present in the data unlike the tree-based models below.

RMSE: 4.039187038714324
R²: 0.8204403180856953

Random Forest

A Random Forest regressor was used to capture nonlinear interactions and relationships between the features. This model substantially improved predictive performance over linear regression.

The feature importance plot shows birth rate, death rate, and a lower estimate of under 5 mortality rate as dominant predictors.

RMSE: 2.6943475436091515
R²: 0.920103431255078

XGBoost

Gradient boosting (XGBoost) achieved the best overall performance, showing that sequential tree construction can be effective at minimizing residual error.

The feature importance plot shows birth rate, a lower estimate of under 5 mortality rate, and a lower estimate of infant mortality rate as the dominant predictors.

RMSE: 2.5922504551087986
R²: 0.9260437608741365

Summary

Insights from a brief investigation of global health data

This was a look at global health data to identify important socioeconomic and public health factors affecting life expectancy. The EDA revealed strong correlations between life expectancy and various indicators such as birth rate, mortality rate, income, age demographics, macronutrients, and access to quality care.

Linear regression showed promising predictive capabilities, but tree based models provided substantial improvements. XGBoost resulted in the lowest errors when taking into account RMSE and R². Based on the feature importances from the random forest and XGBoost models, birth rate and some form of mortality rate were among the leading predictors of life expectancy.

Some limitations included missing data and relatively limited model selection. The dataset was uniformly distributed with respect to year and gender, but it contained a significant amount of missing data, and 90 columns that had over 50% missing values were dropped. As a result, there may have been bias as many socioeconomic factors were excluded. Additionally, while tree-based models improved accuracy, they sacrificed the simplicity of linear regression.

Another path to take would be to explore trends within a single year like 2019 to avoid confounding from life expectancy trends over time and to simplify comparison between countries. To address the issues, future directions could include more advanced imputation methods to retain more features, hyperparameter tuning for the models, and exploring additional algorithms such as neural networks.
